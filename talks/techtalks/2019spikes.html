Getting the Most Out of Your Spikes

Stephen Rollins

10-01-19

http://srollins-lucid.github.io/talks/techtalks/2019spikes.html

What is a Spike?

An exploratory task meant to gather knowledge to open the way for future work with a small amount of focused effort

A way to quickly investigate the current status of a feature with a timeboxed commitment

An opportunity for rapid prototyping, allowing the team to gain traction and determine the best way forward

What is a Spike?

  • Story that results in information, not code
  • Intended to resolve uncertainty and make future estimates more accurate
  • Usually timeboxed

What Isn't a Spike?

  • Not an MVP
  • Not code that users will ever see (can be rough prototype)
  • Not an opportunity to slack off with no deliverable after the story
  • Should not last more than 1-2 "ideal" days

When Should We Do Spikes?

  • To increase the accuracy of an estimate
    • Ideal for large stories, essential for epics
  • When deciding between multiple technologies or approaches

Types of Spikes

Roadmap Spikes

Questions to be Answered

  • How long will this epic take?
  • What steps are required for MVP? What steps are optional?
  • What are the highest-priority tasks to get finished first?
  • How can this epic be coordinated/parallelized across the team?
  • Roughly how complex are the individual tasks?

Example Approach

  1. Understand the Feature Under Investigation
  2. List High-Level Milestones
  3. Break Milestones into Many Component Stories
  4. Understand Environment Capabilities and Limitations

1. Understand the Feature Under Investigation

  • What functionality is required?
  • Where in the product will this code/feature live?
  • How are users likely to use this feature?

2. List High-Level Milestones

  • Major features of the epic
  • Major obstacles or refactorings that are expected

3. Break Milestones into Many Component Stories

  • What steps are required to complete each milestone?
  • Continue breaking stories down until every story can't be reasonably broken into smaller steps

4. Understand Environment Capabilities and Limitations

  • What classes or functions will you need to work with?
  • What third party systems will be involved?
    • Find documentation, and document it
  • What existing code can be leveraged?

4. Understand Environment Capabilities and Limitations (Continued)

  • Consider frontend needs (build targets, i18n, UX work)
  • Consider backend needs (endpoints, data models)
  • Consider database needs (Will new tables be necessary?)
  • Consider infrastructure needs (chef changes, lucidbag changes, new services)

Functional Prototype Spikes

Questions to be Answered

  • How feasible is this project?
  • What steps are required for this project?
  • How long will the remainder of the project take?

Example Approach

  1. Understand the Feature Under Investigation
  2. Dive in with Coding, Hackathon-Style
  3. Don't Spend Too Long on Obstacles
  4. Iterate and Restart
  5. Emphasize a Proof of Concept

1. Understand the Feature Under Investigation

  • What functionality is required?
  • Where in the product will this code/feature live?
  • How are users likely to use this feature?

2. Dive in with Coding, Hackathon-Style

  • Seek to understand how to approach the problem rather than to write perfect code

3. Don't Spend Too Long on Obstacles

  • Spare a brief investigation for problems, but no more
  • Don't let obstacles derail the spike
  • Uncovering deep problems is a good thing--record it and move on
    • Return if there is more time later

4. Iterate and Restart

  • Try multiple approaches to find a good solution
  • Record and retain learnings between iterations
  • Every implementation is an experiment

5. Emphasize a Proof of Concept

  • Full coverage of the feature is more valuable than near-finished code
  • After PoC is achieved, emphasize laying a solid groundwork

Technical/Tooling Spikes

Questions to be Answered

  • What technology, tool, or integration is the best fit for our current need?

Example Approaches

  • Google searching about considered tools
  • Asking informed coworkers
  • Do a Functional Prototype Spike of a handful of selected tools

Doing Spikes Well

Identify deliverable at the start Have a solid deliverable

Deliverables

Confluence Documentation

  • Ideal if specific stories aren't necessary
  • Include many links and details
    • Could another dev work on this epic without you?

Jira Stories

  • Cover as many features and sub-features as possible
  • Provide rough estimates
  • Include enough info so other devs can work without you

Roadmap Diagrams

  • More visual version of Jira Stories
  • Clearly map out dependencies between features
  • Consider populating with data, points, story progress dynamically

Technical Proof of Concept

  • Share your end product and your recommendation with the team
  • Proofs of Concept should also include one of the previous deliverables

Peer-Reviewing a Spike

  • Hold an in-person review and discussion
  • Review deliverable and look for missed details
    • Could you explain the deliverable to someone else?
    • What questions would still be difficult to answer?
    • If reviewing a junior developer's deliverable, consider doing a mini-spike yourself to make sure nothing was overlooked
